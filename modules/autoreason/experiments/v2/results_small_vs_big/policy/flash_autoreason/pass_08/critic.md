Okay, here's a critical review of the AI policy memo, focusing on identifying problems and constraint violations:

*   **Word Count:** While the editor can't verify, a quick estimate suggests the memo is likely very close to or possibly over the 500-word limit. This needs to be checked precisely.

*   **Scope - Item 3:** "AI-powered tools used to derive trends and insights from internal operational data." This item is extremely vague. What constitutes "internal operational data?" Does this include employee performance data? Customer interaction logs *without* PII? The lack of specificity makes this difficult to enforce and opens the door to unintended consequences.

*   **Permitted Uses - Item 3:** "AI-assisted drafting of internal emails and Slack messages." This is problematic because Slack AI features are explicitly disabled, creating an immediate conflict with the base facts. If all Slack AI features *are* disabled, what does "AI-assisted drafting of...Slack messages" even mean? Is it referring to using an external AI tool to generate text that is then copy-pasted into Slack? If so, the item is poorly worded and doesn't clarify if the prohibition of customer PII applies to this use.

*   **Prohibited Uses - Item 5:** "Using any AI tool other than GitHub Copilot Business without explicit written authorization from the CTO, following a security and legal review." This is very difficult to enforce because it is not possible to detect the usage of unapproved AI tools on employee computers without new tooling. It relies entirely on employees self-reporting, which is unlikely, especially if they know they are violating the policy. The policy states it must be enforceable without new tooling.

*   **Prohibited Uses - Item 6:** "Committing AI-generated code to the codebase without review by an engineer with at least two years of experience at the company". This rule does not work as described; after the code is committed, it is too late to stop license violations. The review *must* happen before a commit. Furthermore, relying on engineers to catch license violations assumes they have legal expertise, which is not generally the case. This policy is not enforceable without specialized training for software engineers and/or tooling.

*   **Enforcement - Item 1:** "Engineering managers will review code commits for license compliance and escalate any potential copyright infringement issues flagged during code review to the Legal Department." This is still problematic, even with the edits. Engineering managers are not legal experts and are unlikely to reliably detect complex copyright issues. The onus is on them to "flag" issues, which is not realistic. This implies they'll be actively searching for copyright issues within code, which requires specialized knowledge they likely don't possess.

*   **Enforcement - General:** The enforcement section relies heavily on existing processes and self-reporting. Given the survey results showing widespread AI tool usage *before* this policy, the likelihood of strict adherence and self-reporting is low. Without a mechanism to *detect* unauthorized AI tool usage or PII being fed into AI tools, the policy is largely unenforceable.

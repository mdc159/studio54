1.  The policy lacks clarity on *how* the Security team will "periodically audit AI tool usage." Without specifying the mechanisms for auditing (e.g., log analysis, employee surveys, code review audits), enforcement effectiveness is questionable. The policy states it must be enforceable *without new tooling*, but doesn't specify how existing tooling enables this auditing.
2.  The "Permitted Uses" section is vague. Item 2 allows "AI features within company-approved platforms" without specifying exactly *which* features are approved. This ambiguity could lead to unintentional policy violations.
3.  The policy doesn't address the risk of *indirect* disclosure of sensitive information. For example, an engineer could paraphrase a database schema instead of directly pasting it, which might still allow an AI to infer sensitive details.
4.  The policy does not address how employees can seek "explicit approval" as required in Prohibited Uses items 3 and 4. Without a defined process or point of contact, these prohibitions become difficult to enforce consistently.
5.  The policy does not define what constitutes "explicit approval from the Engineering leadership and Legal" regarding incompatible open-source licenses. Is this a documented exception process, or an informal verbal sign-off? Ambiguity weakens enforceability.
6.  The policy is silent on the use of AI tools for tasks outside of engineering and sales, yet the 'scope' section implies it applies to all employees. What is the policy for the "50 other" employees?
7.  The policy does not address the process for revoking or modifying the policy. AI is a rapidly evolving area, so the policy will need to be updated. There is no indication of how this can happen.

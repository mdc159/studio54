# Pulse — YC Seed Pitch

---

Every engineering team building a consumer app hits the same wall. Somewhere between shipping their core product and reaching their first million users, they lose three to six months building notification infrastructure. Push, email, SMS, in-app — each channel has its own provider, its own failure modes, its own retry logic. It's plumbing. Nobody wants to build it, but everyone does anyway.

**We're Pulse. We're the notification layer engineering teams shouldn't have to build themselves.**

The existing options are a false choice. AWS SNS is powerful but brutally low-level — you're still stitching channels together yourself. OneSignal is built for marketers, not engineers. So most teams roll their own, burning months of engineering time on a reliability problem they won't discover until it's already hurting retention.

That reliability problem is bigger than it looks. The industry average delivery rate is 94%. That sounds acceptable until you do the math: at one million users, you're silently dropping 60,000 notifications every send. Password resets. Transactional alerts. Time-sensitive updates. Gone — with no indication anything went wrong.

**Pulse delivers at 99.97%. That's not a marginal improvement. That's a different reliability class entirely.**

Those numbers are credible because the team is credible. All three of us are infrastructure engineers. Two came from Twilio. One built core systems at AWS SNS. We didn't study this problem — we operated it at scale most startups will never reach. We built Pulse because we knew exactly what the right version looked like, and it didn't exist.

Eight months in: 180 million notifications delivered per month, 47 paying customers, $38K MRR growing 22% month-over-month. We cross $100K MRR in four months. Usage-based pricing — a tenth of a cent per notification above a 100K free tier — means developers adopt without asking permission, then scale with us as they grow. Bottoms-up motion. Economics that compound as customers do.

Our target is the engineering team at a company between one and fifty million monthly active users: past the point where they'd build this themselves, not yet large enough to staff a dedicated platform team. Every company that scales passes through that window. Most are solving this problem badly right now.

The $2M we're raising goes to two places: two additional infrastructure engineers to accelerate reliability and intelligent routing, and SOC 2 certification. Three companies in our current pipeline cannot sign contracts without it. That certification doesn't open a new market — it converts existing conversations into revenue.

Notification infrastructure doesn't get commoditized. Failure modes are invisible until they're catastrophic. Once a team is embedded, switching costs are high. The customer who adopts Pulse at 500K MAU is still a Pulse customer at 50 million. We're not building a feature — we're building the reliability layer that scales with the internet.

The traction is real. The pipeline is ready. The team has built this before, at the scale our customers are racing toward.

We're raising $2M. Let's talk.

---

**What I'd change — and why:**

The pitch is already strong. The structure is sound, the numbers are specific, and the team credibility is well-established. But it has three weaknesses worth fixing:

**1. The opening buries the cost.** "Three to six months" is mentioned but never converted into dollars. Engineering time is the most expensive resource a startup has. Make the reader feel that cost before you offer the solution.

**2. The market size is implied, never stated.** You describe the target customer precisely but never tell investors how many of them exist or what the revenue opportunity looks like. That's a gap a sophisticated investor will notice.

**3. The closing is confident but passive.** "Let's talk" is fine. "Here's exactly what we need and what happens next" is better. The final paragraph should create momentum, not just openness.

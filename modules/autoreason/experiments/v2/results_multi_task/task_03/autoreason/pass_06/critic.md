## Real Problems with This Proposal

### Fundamental Measurement Issues

**Badge tracking duration requirements are unenforceable.** The policy requires "badge entry before 10 AM, exit after 4 PM" but badge systems only track entry/exit events, not whether someone left for lunch, personal errands, or appointments. An employee could badge in at 9:59 AM, leave at 10:15 AM for a 5-hour doctor's appointment, return at 3:45 PM, and badge out at 4:01 PM while technically meeting the requirements.

**"Full business day (8 hours minimum)" contradicts the badge tracking.** The policy defines required days as 8+ hours but only tracks 6+ hours via badges. This creates two conflicting compliance standards that will generate disputes.

**Engineering's "4 days per year" requirement is practically meaningless.** Missing 50% of required days (2 out of 4) only "triggers a manager conversation" while missing 75% (3 out of 4) requires HR involvement. For a policy claiming to be "evidence-based," the consequences are so minimal they provide no enforcement mechanism.

### Operational Impossibilities

**Reserved desk assignments will fail with actual attendance patterns.** Sales requires 15 desks for Tuesday/Wednesday but the policy allows remote work exceptions "with business justification." When 3-5 salespeople are traveling for client meetings (normal business activity), those desks sit empty while other functions can't use them. The policy creates artificial scarcity.

**Video conferencing in "all meeting rooms" assumes infrastructure that doesn't exist.** Most offices have 4-6 meeting rooms maximum. Retrofitting every room with professional video equipment, plus the IT support to maintain it, typically costs $15,000-25,000 per room and requires dedicated technical staff.

**Cross-department Thursday coordination assumes all departments need weekly coordination.** HR, Finance, and Legal don't typically need weekly face-to-face coordination with Engineering or Sales. The policy forces attendance without demonstrating the business need it claims to be based on.

### EU Requirements Create Legal Problems

**Requiring annual US travel for EU employees may violate labor laws.** Many European countries have strict limitations on mandatory international travel frequency. The policy doesn't account for visa requirements, tax implications of extended US presence, or family/medical situations that prevent travel.

**"Monthly cross-department video sessions during 11 AM - 1 PM EST" conflicts with European lunch schedules.** This is 5-7 PM CET, which overlaps with end-of-workday and dinner time in most European cultures. The policy forces EU employees into overtime or personal time for routine coordination.

### Budget Assumptions Are Disconnected From Reality

**$15,000 badge entry/exit system severely underestimates actual costs.** Enterprise access control systems with reporting capabilities typically cost $40,000-80,000 for installation, plus $8,000-15,000 annually for software licensing and maintenance. The budget assumes a simple door lock rather than an enterprise tracking system.

**Equipment replacement calculations ignore actual usage patterns.** The policy assumes 20% annual laptop replacement (3-year cycle) but doesn't account for damage, theft, or technology refresh needs. Most companies replace 25-35% of laptops annually once you include these factors.

### Performance Management Creates Arbitrary Distinctions

**Different violation thresholds by department have no business justification.** Engineering can miss 50% of required days before HR involvement, while Sales missing 6% of required days (6 out of 104) triggers the same response. The policy doesn't explain why Engineering collaboration is less important than Sales presence.

**"Peer feedback scores on cross-team projects" assumes cross-team projects exist regularly.** Many employees work primarily within their department and rarely collaborate across teams. The metric will be unmeasurable for large portions of the workforce.

### Implementation Timeline Ignores Dependencies

**Badge system installation in 4 weeks is unrealistic.** Enterprise access control systems require security assessments, vendor selection, installation coordination with building management, integration with HR systems, and staff training. This typically takes 12-16 weeks minimum.

**"Manager training on attendance reporting" assumes managers will enforce inconsistent requirements.** Sales managers must track 104 days annually per person while Engineering managers track 4 days annually. The cognitive load and administrative burden are completely different, making consistent enforcement impossible.

### Core Policy Logic Problems

**"Evidence-based requirements" aren't actually evidence-based.** The policy states office presence is "tied to specific business needs" but provides no data showing that Tuesday/Wednesday presence improves sales performance or that Thursday presence improves cross-department coordination. The requirements appear arbitrary.

**Remote work approval process for Sales creates operational bottlenecks.** Requiring 48-hour advance notice for client travel conflicts with how sales actually works - client meetings are often scheduled with 24-48 hours notice, and travel requirements emerge from those meetings. The policy will either be routinely violated or will prevent salespeople from serving clients.

**Success metrics measure policy compliance rather than business outcomes.** "Office utilization rates" and "compliance rates" don't demonstrate whether the hybrid model improves collaboration, productivity, or business results. High compliance with a bad policy still produces bad results.

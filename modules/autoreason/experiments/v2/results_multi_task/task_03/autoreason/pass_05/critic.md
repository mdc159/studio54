## Critical Problems with This Proposal

### Enforcement and Compliance Issues

**Engineering's quarterly requirement is unenforceable.** With only 4 meetings per year, missing even one represents 25% non-compliance, but the policy requires "3+ instances per quarter" for HR escalation. The math doesn't work - you can't have 3+ violations of a quarterly requirement within a single quarter.

**Badge access tracking won't capture actual compliance.** The policy uses "basic badge tracking for office access" but needs to verify people stay for full days, not just badge in and leave. Without detailed tracking, you can't measure compliance with the day-length requirements.

**"Manager approval for remote work requests" creates inconsistent application.** Different Sales managers will have different standards for what constitutes valid "client travel or family emergencies," leading to fairness complaints and potential legal issues.

### Operational Contradictions

**Hot-desking with 1-week booking doesn't support required attendance.** If Sales needs to be in Tuesday/Wednesday, and others need Wednesday, there won't be enough desks available with only one week advance booking. The booking system will become a bottleneck that prevents policy compliance.

**"Remote-first" meetings contradict required office presence.** If meetings default to remote participation, there's no business justification for requiring people to be in the office. The policy undermines its own requirements.

**Core collaboration hours (11 AM - 1 PM EST) don't align with required office days.** EU team members are supposed to follow the same requirements as their functions, but their 5-7 PM participation time doesn't overlap with US office hours when collaboration actually happens.

### Budget and Resource Problems

**$500 home office allowance won't cover listed equipment.** A docking station, external monitor, and headset easily exceed $500 even before the "home office setup" components. The budget is internally inconsistent.

**Office space utilization assumptions are wrong.** The policy assumes current office space can handle hot-desking for required days, but doesn't account for the fact that Sales (2 days/week) + Others (1 day/week on Wednesday) creates peak demand that likely exceeds current capacity.

### Missing Implementation Details

**No definition of "day" length.** Policy requires specific days in office but doesn't specify whether that means 8 hours, 6 hours, or just "some presence." This creates immediate disputes.

**Virtual participation for EU team is undefined.** How do you "virtually participate" in "whiteboarding sessions" or "equipment setup"? The policy lists activities that can't be done remotely but then says EU team can participate virtually.

**Performance evaluation lacks measurable criteria.** "Communication effectiveness" and "required office presence compliance" aren't defined with specific metrics, making consistent evaluation impossible.

### Structural Design Flaws

**Department-specific requirements create internal equity problems.** Engineering gets quarterly requirements while Sales gets 2 days/week. Even if business-justified, this creates a 26x difference in office requirements that will generate ongoing resentment and retention issues.

**Policy governance timeline is misaligned.** Monthly manager feedback on a quarterly Engineering requirement means 2 months of no data to review. The feedback cycles don't match the requirement cycles.

**Success metrics don't measure policy objectives.** The policy aims to address "collaboration needs" but measures "office space utilization" and "response time compliance" instead of actual collaboration effectiveness or business outcomes.

**Equipment replacement assumption is arbitrary.** 5% annual replacement rate has no basis in actual equipment lifecycle data and doesn't account for different replacement needs across equipment types (laptops vs. headsets vs. monitors).

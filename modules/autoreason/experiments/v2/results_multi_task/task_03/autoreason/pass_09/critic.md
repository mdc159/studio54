## Critical Problems

### Engineering Requirements Are Unenforceable
The "2 full-day planning sessions per quarter" requirement creates an impossible compliance measurement problem. What constitutes "collaborative work" for 6+ hours? Who determines if architecture decisions actually required in-person presence? The policy creates subjective standards that will lead to disputes between engineers and managers about whether attendance "counted."

### Sales Travel Exception Creates Massive Loophole
"No advance approval required for client meetings requiring travel" with only weekly schedule submission is completely unverifiable. Sales reps can claim any absence as "client business" and submit retroactive justifications. The policy has no mechanism to distinguish legitimate client travel from personal time or job interviews.

### Desk Reservation Math Doesn't Work
25 desks for 50 employees assumes perfect scheduling coordination that ignores human behavior. When Tuesday/Wednesday are mandatory for Sales (15 people) plus Engineering quarterly sessions (20 people), you need 35+ desks on peak days. The reservation system will fail immediately, creating conflict and policy abandonment.

### Video Meeting Infrastructure Will Collapse Under Load
4 video rooms for 50 employees with mandatory 48-hour advance booking creates a scheduling bottleneck. Engineering quarterly sessions alone could consume all video room capacity for entire weeks. The "backup protocol" of moving to all-remote defeats the entire purpose of required in-office attendance.

### Performance Standards Are Completely Subjective
"Collaboration Quality: Project completion rates and stakeholder satisfaction" has no measurement methodology. How do you separate collaboration quality from project complexity, market conditions, or resource constraints? "Stakeholder satisfaction" isn't defined - internal stakeholders, clients, or both?

### EU Timezone Window Is Operationally Useless
10 AM - 12 PM EST (4 PM - 6 PM CET) overlap assumes EU employees work until 6 PM daily, which violates typical European work schedules. Two hours isn't sufficient for meaningful project coordination across time zones.

### Budget Assumptions Are Divorced From Reality
$1,500 laptop budget "including setup and shipping" ignores enterprise procurement overhead, IT configuration time, security compliance requirements, and international shipping costs for EU employees. The actual cost per employee will be significantly higher.

### Compliance Tracking Has No Actual Mechanism
"Manager observation through team interaction" provides no objective data for attendance verification. Managers can't observe who's physically present without being physically present themselves. The policy creates an enforcement gap that makes the requirements meaningless.

### Missing Critical Infrastructure Dependencies
The policy assumes existing IT infrastructure can handle 50 remote employees accessing corporate systems simultaneously. No mention of VPN capacity, security protocols for home networks, or bandwidth requirements for video conferencing.

### Performance Issue Process Lacks Authority Structure
"Collaborative problem-solving to address barriers" has no escalation path when employees simply refuse to comply. The policy doesn't specify who has authority to modify individual arrangements or what happens when "collaborative" solutions fail.

### Implementation Timeline Ignores Procurement Reality
8-week equipment procurement and setup timeline assumes vendors have inventory, IT can configure 50 devices simultaneously, and shipping faces no delays. Enterprise procurement typically takes 12-16 weeks for this scale.

### Success Metrics Can't Be Measured
"Cross-team project success rates" assumes projects can be clearly categorized as collaborative vs. individual work and that success/failure can be attributed to work location rather than other factors. Most business outcomes have multiple variables that make location correlation impossible to isolate.

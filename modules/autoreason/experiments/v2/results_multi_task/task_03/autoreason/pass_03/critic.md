## Critical Problems with This Proposal

### Fundamental Design Contradictions

**Engineering "Remote-First" vs. Required Office Days**: The policy claims to accommodate Engineering's "remote-first preferences" while mandating 2 consecutive days monthly plus quarterly sessions. This isn't remote-first—it's hybrid with lower frequency. The contradiction will create immediate pushback.

**"All participants join via video platform (even in-office attendees)"**: This defeats the entire purpose of requiring people to be in the office. If everyone's on video calls anyway, why force physical presence? The policy undermines its own rationale.

**Function-Specific Requirements Based on "Actual Role Demands"**: No evidence is provided that Sales actually needs 3 days/week in office while Engineering needs only 2 days/month. These appear to be arbitrary distinctions without supporting analysis.

### Unworkable Operational Mechanics

**Badge Swipe Enforcement for Monthly Requirements**: Tracking 2 days per month via badge swipes creates a compliance nightmare. What happens if someone badges in but leaves early? Works from the office but doesn't badge in? The granularity doesn't match the enforcement mechanism.

**"Scheduled 2 weeks in advance" for Engineering**: This assumes perfect predictability of work schedules and personal lives. Real software development has urgent issues, sick days, family emergencies, and shifting priorities that make rigid scheduling unrealistic.

**EU Team "Virtual Participation Only" Exception**: This creates a two-tier system where EU employees are exempt from requirements that US employees must follow. This will breed resentment and questions about fairness, especially if EU employees advance at similar rates.

### Complexity That Doesn't Pay for Itself

**Alternating All-Hands Meeting Times**: The administrative overhead of tracking and scheduling alternating times, ensuring all employees know which time applies each month, and managing the confusion when people miss the rotation isn't worth the marginal timezone accommodation.

**Department-Specific Performance Metrics**: The policy creates three different evaluation frameworks (Engineering, Sales, Other) that require separate training, tracking systems, and management processes. HR will struggle to maintain consistency and fairness across these different standards.

**30-Day Improvement Plan Process**: This creates a bureaucratic process that requires documentation, HR consultation, weekly check-ins, and review cycles—all to address what might be simple scheduling conflicts or misunderstandings.

### Missing Critical Implementation Details

**No Definition of "Consecutive Days"**: Does this mean Monday-Tuesday? Thursday-Friday? Can it span a weekend? Without clarity, employees and managers will interpret this differently, leading to conflicts.

**"Pre-approved Remote Work Requests" for Sales**: No process, criteria, or approval authority is specified. Sales managers don't know when to approve or deny requests, creating inconsistent application.

**Badge Swipe System Technical Requirements**: The policy assumes badge systems can accurately track partial days, distinguish between work presence and brief visits, and generate meaningful reports. Most badge systems aren't designed for this granularity.

### Unrealistic Assumptions

**5-Minute Technical Buffer**: Assumes all technical issues can be resolved in 5 minutes. Real hybrid meeting problems (audio feedback, screen sharing failures, network issues) often take much longer and disrupt the entire meeting flow.

**$1,000 Home Office Allowance Adequacy**: This amount may be insufficient in high cost-of-living areas or excessive in others. The flat rate ignores regional economic differences and actual furniture costs.

**Weekly EU-US Coordination Sessions**: Assumes there's always meaningful coordination work to fill weekly sessions. Many weeks may have minimal cross-timezone dependencies, making these meetings wasteful.

### Enforcement and Compliance Gaps

**"Performance Issues Attributed to Communication"**: No clear criteria for determining when missed deadlines are communication-related versus other factors (scope changes, technical challenges, resource constraints). This creates subjective enforcement.

**Office Space "70-85% Utilization Target"**: No mechanism specified for measuring utilization of collaboration spaces versus individual desks, or how to adjust when utilization falls outside target ranges.

**Security Audit Feasibility**: Quarterly security audits of home offices is logistically complex and potentially invasive. No process defined for conducting these audits or consequences for non-compliance.

### Budget and Resource Problems

**EU Vendor Contract Costs**: $20,000 for EU vendor contracts appears arbitrary without knowing what services are needed or market rates. This could be massively under or over-budgeted.

**Office Reconfiguration During Active Use**: Converting 15 desks to 8 collaboration spaces while people are still required to use the office creates workspace shortage during the transition period.

**Annual Equipment Refresh Calculation**: $300 annually per employee assumes specific replacement cycles and costs that may not reflect actual equipment lifespans or failure rates.

The policy attempts to solve too many problems simultaneously without validating core assumptions about what actually drives productivity and collaboration in each function.

## Critical Problems with This Proposal

### Engineering Requirements Are Still Meaningless
8 days per year (2 days per quarter) is still practically nothing. "Missing 5+ sessions requires formal improvement plan" means an engineer could skip 62% of required sessions (5 out of 8) and only get a performance conversation. This creates no real incentive for participation.

### Quarterly Planning Sessions Assume Wrong Work Patterns
The policy assumes all engineering work requires quarterly big-room planning sessions. Many engineering teams work in continuous sprints with weekly planning. The quarterly model may not align with actual development cycles, making these sessions feel artificial and disconnected from real work.

### Sales Travel Exception Creates Massive Loophole
"Submit travel schedule weekly showing client meetings" has no verification mechanism. Sales people can claim any activity as "client business" with no oversight. The 24-hour advance notice for other situations is meaningless when 80% of remote work can be justified as travel.

### Shared Desk Math Doesn't Work
25 desks for 50 employees assumes perfect distribution. If Sales (15 people) needs to be in Tuesday/Wednesday, that's potentially 15 desks needed just for Sales on those days. Add other functions (15 people) and you're already at 30 desks needed, exceeding capacity. The reservation system will fail on high-demand days.

### Simple Online Calendar for Desk Reservations Will Break
"Day-of or day-before" reservations with "no penalties for unreserved desk usage" creates chaos. People will show up without reservations, find no desks, and either go home or displace someone else. There's no enforcement mechanism described.

### Equipment Budget Ignores Procurement Reality
$1,800 per employee assumes bulk purchasing power and immediate availability. Laptop shortages, supply chain issues, and corporate procurement processes will stretch timelines and inflate costs. The budget doesn't account for shipping, setup time, or IT support labor.

### Video Equipment in Only 2 Rooms Creates Bottleneck
With hybrid meetings as the default, 2 video-equipped rooms for 50 employees is insufficient. The 48-hour advance booking requirement means most meetings will be forced into non-video rooms, undermining the hybrid meeting policy.

### Manager Observation of Attendance Patterns Is Subjective
"Manager awareness of attendance patterns through observation" provides no consistent measurement. Different managers will interpret and report patterns differently. There's no standardized definition of what constitutes a concerning pattern.

### Core Collaboration Hours Still Exclude EU Team
10 AM - 12 PM EST is 4 PM - 6 PM CET, which is end-of-workday in Europe. The EU person will be trying to leave work when US teams want collaboration. This doesn't actually solve the timezone problem.

### Performance Evaluation Metrics Are Unmeasurable
"Stakeholder satisfaction for collaborative work" assumes you can identify which work is collaborative vs. individual. "Project completion rates" doesn't account for project complexity differences. These metrics can't be consistently measured across different roles.

### Equipment Replacement Rate Is Arbitrary
30% annual replacement rate has no basis in actual usage data. The policy doesn't define what constitutes damage vs. normal wear, or whether employees can request upgrades vs. replacements.

### Implementation Timeline Ignores Dependencies
Installing video equipment in Week 1-8 assumes rooms are already identified, network infrastructure exists, and equipment is available. Setting up desk reservation systems assumes IT resources are available and requirements are defined. These tasks have dependencies not accounted for in the timeline.

### Policy Amendment Authority Is Unclear
"Leadership team approval" and "Department head approval" don't specify who exactly has authority, what constitutes approval, or how conflicts between departments get resolved.

### Missing Enforcement Mechanisms
The policy describes what should happen but not who enforces it or how. There's no process for addressing violations, no escalation path, and no consequences for managers who don't track attendance patterns.

### Success Metrics Can't Be Attributed to Policy
"Project completion rates" and "revenue per employee" are influenced by dozens of factors beyond work location. You can't determine if changes in these metrics are caused by the hybrid policy or other business factors.

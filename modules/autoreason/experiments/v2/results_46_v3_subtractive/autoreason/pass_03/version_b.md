# Notification System Design Proposal
## Social App — 10M MAU, 4 Engineers, 6 Months
### Revision 4

---

## Executive Summary

This proposal designs a notification system handling ~50M notifications/day across push, email, in-app, and SMS channels. Given the team size and timeline, we prioritize **operational simplicity over theoretical elegance** and **incremental delivery** over a big-bang launch.

The core architectural bet: **a single priority queue with channel fanout**, rather than per-channel queues or a complex event streaming topology. This is debuggable, replaceable, and sufficient for 10M MAU with a team of 4.

**What changed in Revision 4:** Corrected FCM v1 batching (single-device API, not multicast); fixed the traffic model to use consistent recipient bases; specified preference cache TTL and invalidation strategy; replaced Redis Pub/Sub with Redis Streams for WebSocket fan-out; addressed SES duplicate-send risk in digest idempotency; defined Redis Stream consumer group semantics for the token invalidation path; replaced the 90-day passive purge with active invalid-token cleanup; resolved the low-activity user digest failure; added worker pool sizing via Little's Law; completed all sections including partition maintenance, §4–§7.

## 1. Scale Assumptions and Constraints

### Traffic Modeling

The previous version mixed two incompatible recipient bases: it derived total volume from DAU×rate, then attributed push volume to MAU. These are inconsistent. This version uses a single model: notifications are generated by events on the platform, and recipients are drawn from the relevant active population per channel.

**Volume derivation:**

| Metric | Estimate | Basis |
|--------|----------|-------|
| MAU | 10M | Given |
| DAU | 3M | 30% DAU/MAU (social apps) |
| Social events/DAU/day | ~17 | Likes, comments, follows, mentions |
| Raw notification events/day | ~51M | 3M × 17 |
| After preference filtering (~2% opt-out) | ~50M | Rounded working figure |
| Peak multiplier | 3× | Morning/evening spikes |
| Peak throughput | ~1,750/sec | 50M × 3 / 86,400 |

**Channel split — consistent recipient bases:**

Each channel's volume derives from the same 50M daily notification events, distributed by the delivery channel selected after preference resolution. Recipients are not re-derived per channel from different population bases.

| Channel | % of Routed Volume | Volume/day | Notes |
|---------|-------------------|------------|-------|
| Push | 70% | 35M | Sent to MAU with push tokens (~8M); many events generate push even for users not currently active |
| In-app | 20% | 10M | Written for DAU (3M); near-duplicate of push for active sessions, serves as persistent inbox |
| Email | 8% | 4M | Batched into digests for MAU with email (~9M); not 4M individual sends |
| SMS | <0.01% | ~2,000 | Auth/security only; hard budget ceiling |

**Why push volume (35M) can exceed DAU×rate while total volume stays at 50M:** Push notifications are routed to 8M MAU with installed apps, not just the 3M daily active users. A user who hasn't opened the app today still receives push for high-priority events. The 50M total is the event count; the 35M push figure is the subset of those events routed to the push channel across all eligible recipients. These numbers are consistent — they don't add up to more than 50M because channel percentages are of total routed volume, not independent calculations.

**SMS cost ceiling:** At Twilio's $0.0075/message, 1M SMS/day = $225,000/month. Our SMS budget ceiling is ~$15,000/month, which caps us at ~2,000 SMS/day. SMS is strictly authentication codes and security alerts.

| Engineer | Primary Responsibility | Cross-Cutting Owner |
|----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| E1 | Core pipeline, queue infrastructure, delivery workers | Aggregation/batching logic; worker pool sizing |

Neither engineer makes cross-domain writes directly. E2's workers publish structured `token.invalidated` events to a Redis Stream; E3's feedback processor consumes them via a named consumer group and writes suppression records. Consumer group semantics are specified in §6.3.

## 2. System Architecture

### High-Level Data Flow

```
Event Sources
     │
     ▼
[Event Ingestion API]
     │
     ▼
[Notification Router]
  - Preference check (Redis cache, TTL=5min, write-through invalidation)
  - Suppression check
  - Priority assignment
  - Channel selection
  - Aggregation window check
     │
     ├─────────────────────────────────────┐
     ▼                                     ▼
[Priority Queue]                   [In-App Store]
(Redis sorted set, Lua-atomic)     (PostgreSQL, partitioned)
     │                                     │
     ├── P0 Worker Pool (20)               └── Redis Streams fan-out
     ├── P1 Worker Pool (40)                   → WebSocket servers
     └── P2/P3 Worker Pool (20)                (acknowledged delivery)
          │
          ▼
   [Channel Dispatcher]
     ├── Push (APNs / FCM — per-device sends)
     ├── Email (Postmark transactional / SES digest)
     └── SMS (Twilio — auth/security only)
          │
          ▼
   [Delivery Log] (PostgreSQL + S3 archive)
          │
          ▼
   [Feedback Processor]  ←── token.invalidated stream (consumer group: feedback-proc)
   (bounces, opens, failures → suppression)
```

### Why This Architecture

**Single queue with priority scoring, not per-channel queues.** Per-channel queues create operational complexity — 4 queues to monitor, 4 dead-letter queues, priority inversions between channels. At our scale, a Redis sorted set with Lua-atomic dequeue handles 50M items comfortably.

**In-app notifications bypass the queue.** In-app notifications are writes to a database table that clients poll or receive via WebSocket. They don't need external API calls and benefit from immediate consistency. Routing them through the push queue adds latency with no benefit.

**Preference check at routing time, not delivery time.** User preferences are cached in Redis with write-through invalidation (see §4.3). Checking at routing keeps workers dumb and fast. Checking at delivery wastes queue capacity on notifications that will be discarded.

**Honest statement of operational risk.** This system combines Redis sorted sets with Lua atomicity, PostgreSQL declarative partitioning, Redis Streams for WebSocket fan-out, a custom digest engine, a custom feedback loop, and direct APNs/FCM integrations. None of these components is individually exotic. But this team has never operated this combination together at 50M notifications/day. The risk is not in any single component — it's in the operational surface area of running all of them simultaneously. We mitigate this through phased rollout (§7), explicit runbooks (§6), and E4's dedicated reliability ownership.

## 3. Delivery Channels

### 3.1 Push Notifications (70% of volume — 35M/day)

**Provider:** Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) for Android, Apple Push Notification Service (APNs) for iOS. Direct integrations — no intermediary like OneSignal or Braze initially.

**Tradeoff:** OneSignal/Braze saves 4–6 weeks of integration work but costs ~$50–150K/year at our scale and reduces control over retry behavior and delivery receipts. We build direct integrations and accept the upfront engineering cost. We revisit if we need advanced segmentation features.

**FCM — Corrected API Model:**

FCM v1 is a single-device API. There is no v1 batch endpoint accepting multiple tokens. The deprecated legacy FCM HTTP API supported multicast up to 1,000 tokens but is being sunset. We use FCM v1 exclusively.

This has throughput implications. At 35M push/day with 70% going to Android (rough estimate), that's ~24.5M FCM sends/day, or ~284/sec average, ~850/sec at peak. Each send is one HTTP/2 request to FCM.

```
FCM v1 runtime config:
- HTTP/2 multiplexed connections: 100 persistent connections
  (HTTP/2 allows multiple concurrent streams per connection)
- Target: 10 concurrent requests per connection = 1,000 concurrent in-flight
- FCM project-level quota: 600,000 requests/minute (~10,000/sec)
  We operate at ≤1,000/sec, well within quota
- On 404 (Unregistered) or InvalidRegistration:
    → publish token.invalidated event to Redis Stream immediately
    → do not retry this token
- On 429 (quota exceeded): exponential backoff, alert E4
- On 500/503: retry with backoff up to 3 attempts, then DLQ
```

**APNs Configuration and JWT Token Management:**

APNs uses `.p8` signing keys to generate short-lived JWT tokens. Three distinct layers:

- **`.p8` signing key:** Does not rotate on a schedule. Stored in AWS Secrets Manager. Rotated manually via Apple Developer Portal only if compromised. E2 owns the rotation runbook.
- **JWT token:** Generated from the `.p8` key. Expires after 1 hour. Regenerated every 45 minutes to stay well inside the expiry window.
- **In-flight requests during regeneration:** Workers fetch the token per-request from a local cache with a 30-second TTL. During regeneration, workers may use a token up to 30 seconds old — still valid since we regenerate at 45 minutes against a 60-minute expiry. No requests are interrupted.

**APNs JWT regeneration — operational design:**

Failure here means complete iOS push delivery failure after the current token expires. It requires the same monitoring rigor as a primary service.

```
Monitoring (owned by E4):
- Heartbeat metric emitted after every successful regeneration
- Alert: PagerDuty page if no successful regeneration in 50 minutes
- Alert: APNs 401 error rate > 1% triggers immediate page
  (401 = token rejected; indicates regeneration failure or clock skew)
- Dashboard: token age, last regeneration timestamp, 401 rate

On alert:
- Runbook: verify Secrets Manager access, verify background job health,
  manually trigger regeneration if job is stuck
- Fallback: if regeneration fails for >10 minutes, pause iOS push delivery
  and queue notifications for retry rather than send with an expired token
```

```
APNs runtime config:
- HTTP/2 with JWT authentication (token regenerated every 45 min)
- Connection pool: 20 persistent connections
- apns-priority: 10 (immediate) for P0/P1, 5 (power-saving) for P2
- apns-collapse-id: notification_type + entity_id (collapses stale notifications)
- On 410: publish token.invalidated event with Apple-provided timestamp
```

**Token lifecycle — active cleanup replaces passive purge:**

The previous version batch-purged tokens unused for 90 days. This is inadequate: APNs and FCM both return explicit invalidity signals immediately when a token is invalid. Waiting 90 days means sending to dead tokens for up to 90 days, inflating error rates and wasting API quota. FCM tracks per-project error rates; sustained high invalidity rates can affect deliverability.

**Revised approach: act on invalidity signals immediately, use time-based purge only as a backstop.**

```
Token lifecycle:
1. On FCM 404/InvalidRegistration or APNs 410:
   - Publish token.invalidated event to Redis Stream within the delivery worker
   - E3's feedback processor marks token invalid within seconds (see §6.3)
   - No further sends to this token from that point forward

2. APNs 410 timestamp handling:
   - Apple provides the timestamp of when the token became invalid
   - Tokens registered for the same device AFTER that timestamp are valid
   - Feedback processor records the invalidity timestamp and only suppresses
     tokens with registered_at <= apple_invalidity_timestamp

3. Time-based backstop (reduced from 90 to 30 days):
   - Batch-purge tokens with last_used_at > 30 days AND no recent invalidity signal
   - This catches uninstalls on platforms that don't return explicit signals
   - 30 days is aggressive enough to limit sustained sends to dead tokens
   - Nightly job, owned by E4

4. Proactive validation (new):
   - Weekly: attempt a silent push (content-available: 1, no alert) to tokens
     unused in the past 14 days
   - Invalidity signals from these probes trigger immediate suppression
   - Cost: minimal (silent pushes don't wake the device or show UI)
```

### 3.2 Email (8% of volume — 4M events/day, not 4M individual sends)

**Clarification on volume:** The 4M/day figure is notification events routed to email, not individual email sends. Email is delivered as digests (daily or weekly) for most users. Actual send volume is much lower — roughly 1M–2M emails/day depending on digest frequency distribution.

**Provider selection — cost-honest analysis:**

At ~120M emails/month, actual costs:

| Provider | Estimated Cost/Month | Deliverability Tooling | Ops Burden |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------|------------|
| SendGrid (enterprise) | ~$20,000–40,000 | Excellent | Low |
| AWS SES | ~$12,000 | Minimal | High |
| Postmark | ~$15,000–25,000 | Good | Low–Medium |
| Mailgun | ~$15,000–20,000 | Good | Low–Medium |

**Recommendation: Postmark for transactional, SES for digest/marketing.** Postmark has strong deliverability for transactional email (password resets, security alerts) where failure has direct user trust impact. SES handles high-volume digest sends at lower cost. Tradeoff: E2 maintains two email integrations.

**Email types:**

| Type | Provider | Trigger | Frequency Cap |
|------|---------|---------|---------------|
| Transactional | Postmark | Immediate (password reset, account alert) | No cap |
| Activity digest | SES | Daily or weekly batch | User-controlled |
| Re-engagement | SES | 7-day inactivity | Max 1/week |
| Marketing | SES | Product announcements | Max 1/week |

**Digest batching logic — corrected idempotency and failure handling:**

The previous version claimed `digest_id` provides idempotency, but SES does not support idempotency keys. A retry after a network timeout produces a duplicate email. We address this with a two-phase commit pattern: record the send attempt before sending, and use that record to detect and suppress duplicates on retry.

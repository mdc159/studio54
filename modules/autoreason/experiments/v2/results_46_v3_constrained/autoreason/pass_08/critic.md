Here are the real problems:

**Market and competitive positioning**

The claim that competitors are "workflow-first" and Pulse is "engineer-first" is a positioning assertion, not a demonstrated structural difference. Knock and Novu both have SDKs, APIs, and programmatic delivery control. The pitch doesn't explain what Pulse actually does differently at the architecture level — it just claims superiority and cites a few customer complaints about a competitor. That's not a moat analysis.

**The traction numbers are weak for the ask**

$38K MRR after eight months is roughly $800 per customer average. For a product targeting engineering teams at scaling consumer apps, that's a very low ACV. The pitch acknowledges distribution failure but doesn't explain why $2M fixes it — the proposed hire is one enterprise sales person, which is a thin plan for a market they've already demonstrated they can't reach organically.

**The "8,000 US companies" TAM framing is unexamined**

No methodology is given for that number. No revenue per customer estimate. No market sizing math. It's stated as if it's self-evidently meaningful.

**The reliability claims are slippery**

The pitch explicitly says they measure "gateway acceptance plus confirmed downstream delivery where carriers return confirmation signals" — which is not end-to-end delivery. Then it immediately uses that metric to claim a "different reliability class" versus industry average. Comparing their partial metric to a presumably different industry metric is not a valid comparison. The 60,000 dropped notifications figure is presented with false precision built on this shaky foundation.

**SOC2 as a fundraise rationale is a red flag**

Framing SOC2 completion as the primary use of seed capital means they're raising money to fix a process failure they admit was their own mistake. Investors are being asked to fund the correction of a known self-inflicted wound, not to accelerate a working motion.

**The "first twelve customers haven't churned" claim**

Six months of retention in a small, self-selected early adopter cohort is presented as validation of an expansion model. These are likely the customers who had the most patience and the most direct relationship with the founders. It says almost nothing about what happens at 47 customers, let alone 470.

**The Series A thesis is not actually specific**

"Get into active evaluation at ten enterprise accounts, close two" — this is a sales activity target, not a business milestone. It doesn't specify deal size, doesn't define what "active evaluation" means, and doesn't connect to a revenue number that would make a Series A logical.

**The Twilio/AWS pedigree claim**

Mentioned once, not substantiated. Which engineers, what systems, what scale? This is the kind of credential that should do real work in a pitch and it's just asserted.

**The switching cost argument is premature**

The claim that deeply instrumented customers won't churn is made about a product that has been in market eight months with 47 customers. There is no evidence of switching costs yet — there's a theory of switching costs. Presenting it as a known retention dynamic is unsupported.

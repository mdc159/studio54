# Pulse — YC Seed Pitch (Revised)

---

Every engineering team building a consumer app hits the same wall. Somewhere between shipping their core product and scaling to their first million users, they lose three to six months building notification infrastructure. Push, email, SMS, in-app — each channel has its own provider, its own failure modes, its own retry logic. Nobody wants to build it. Everyone does anyway.

We're Pulse. We built the notification layer engineering teams shouldn't have to build themselves.

The competitive landscape is real but specific. AWS SNS is a single channel — you're still stitching the rest together yourself. OneSignal is built for marketers. Knock and Novu have APIs, but their core primitive is a workflow: a template, a trigger, a send. Ours is a delivery guarantee: configurable retry trees per channel, fallback routing when a gateway degrades, and per-notification status that engineers can query programmatically. The structural difference is that our architecture was designed around failure modes first, not UI configuration first. Marcus and Jen between them spent six years at Twilio building carrier retry logic and four years at AWS SNS running the delivery pipeline at hundreds of billions of notifications annually. That's not a credential line — that's the reason our retry architecture exists at all rather than being bolted on later.

On delivery reliability: we measure gateway acceptance plus confirmed downstream delivery where carriers return signals. We won't claim omniscience across all platforms — nobody has that. What we can measure precisely is confirmed failures, and our rate is 0.03% against an industry figure that aggregates differently. We'll show you the raw data and the methodology side by side and let you assess the comparison yourself. What we can say without qualification: customers migrating to us report fewer missed transactional alerts. For a million-user app, even a conservative improvement in that failure rate is 60,000 fewer dropped notifications per send cycle. For transactional alerts — account access, payment confirmation, delivery status — that's the difference between infrastructure that works and infrastructure that apologizes.

Eight months in: 47 paying customers, $38K MRR, 180 million notifications monthly. We'll be direct about what that means. $800 average MRR per customer is low for this market, and it reflects a real failure: we built like infrastructure engineers and assumed product quality would pull customers in. It doesn't, at this stage. The signal we do have is clean: our first twelve customers have expanded spend as their user bases grew, none have churned, and the usage-based model means revenue grows with their scale. That's not proof of a retention moat — it's eight months of early data that the expansion logic holds where we can observe it. Three enterprise deals are currently stalled on SOC2. That's a self-inflicted mistake we're correcting.

We're raising $2M with specific sequencing. SOC2 first — it closes known revenue and is table stakes for the segment we're targeting. Then one enterprise sales hire with a specific profile: someone who has sold developer infrastructure before, not a growth generalist, because the motion for selling to engineering teams is different and we've already seen what happens without it. Then one senior infrastructure engineer. Our Series A bar is concrete: $300K MRR, two enterprise contracts above $5K monthly, and expansion revenue from the current cohort exceeding new logo revenue. Eighteen months is enough time to find out honestly whether we can hit it.

We're asking for $2M to become the default notification layer for scaling consumer apps. The market is real, the problem is structural, and the team has built these systems before at the companies that defined the category.

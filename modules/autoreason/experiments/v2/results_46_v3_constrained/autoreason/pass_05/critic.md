Here are the real problems:

**The core reliability claim is self-undermining**

The pitch spends a paragraph walking back the 99.97% delivery rate, admitting it's gateway acceptance plus confirmed delivery "where platforms return confirmation." This is not a delivery rate. It's a partial measurement. But then the pitch uses the number anyway — "60,000 dropped notifications per send" — as if the caveat didn't just hollow it out. The comparison to an "industry 94% average" is equally unsubstantiated. If you can't actually measure end-to-end delivery, neither can your competitors, so the comparison is apples to something undefined.

**The differentiation doesn't hold under pressure**

"Infrastructure-first" versus "workflow-first" is a positioning claim, not a technical moat. Knock, Novu, and Courier all have API-first integration paths. The pitch doesn't explain what Pulse has built that those companies couldn't add in a quarter. Engineers who "instrument in an afternoon and never think about it again" is also exactly what those competitors promise.

**47 customers and $38K MRR after 8 months is weak for the market size claimed**

If there are 8,000 target companies in the US alone, and the product is genuinely superior, 47 customers suggests something is wrong beyond "we underinvested in distribution." The pitch doesn't grapple with this seriously.

**The SOC2 framing deflects a deeper problem**

Three enterprise deals stalled on SOC2 sounds fixable. But an infrastructure company selling to engineering teams at companies with real scale that didn't pursue SOC2 from the start suggests either the founders didn't understand enterprise sales at all, or those deals aren't as far along as implied. SOC2 is table stakes — its absence this late is a red flag about judgment, not just execution.

**"Our oldest cohorts are holding" is doing a lot of work**

This is the entire churn story, and it's vague. How old? What's the actual retention curve? "Holding" could mean anything. For a company whose expansion revenue model depends on customers growing into higher tiers, the absence of concrete cohort data here is conspicuous.

**The $2M ask and 18-month runway math is thin**

SOC2 Type II, one senior infrastructure engineer, one head of growth, and 18 months of runway on $2M from a $38K MRR base means burn is low — but the Series A story depends on closing enterprise contracts and building a repeatable sales motion. That's a lot of sequential dependencies with no slack. If SOC2 takes longer, or the head of growth takes two quarters to produce pipeline, the story breaks.

**The target customer band is a transit zone, not a home**

The pitch acknowledges that customers who grow past this band eventually staff platform teams — and frames this as a retention positive because they "don't rip out infrastructure that works." That's unproven and optimistic. Large companies routinely consolidate vendors, bring things in-house, or get acquired. The 1–50M MAU band is also where companies are most likely to be cost-sensitive and engineering-capable enough to evaluate alternatives seriously.

**"Built by engineers who ran these systems at Twilio and AWS SNS" is unverified**

This is a significant credibility claim presented without any supporting detail. Who, specifically? What did they build? In a pitch that otherwise tries to be precise and honest, this reads as a credential drop that invites scrutiny it doesn't satisfy.

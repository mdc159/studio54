Here are real problems with this proposal:

**Market and Competition**

The claim that competitors are "workflow-first" and built for product managers is asserted, not demonstrated. Knock and Courier have extensive developer APIs and programmatic control. The distinction being drawn may not be as sharp as claimed, and sophisticated investors will know this.

The "8,000 US companies in our target band" figure appears without definition. What band? Revenue? Headcount? Notification volume? This number is doing a lot of work in the pitch and is completely unsubstantiated.

**Traction and Growth**

$38K MRR from 47 customers after eight months is weak — roughly $800 average contract value. For a product targeting engineering teams at scaling companies, this suggests either very small customers or significant discounting, neither of which supports the enterprise framing.

180 million notifications monthly sounds impressive but is not contextualized. What's the revenue per notification? What does this imply about pricing power?

The pitch openly admits distribution has failed for eight months. Investors are being asked to fund the fix to a founder-identified execution failure, with no evidence the team can execute a different motion than the one that already failed.

**Reliability Claims**

The "60,000 dropped notifications" comparison is constructed from their own confirmed failure rate versus an unnamed "industry average." The methodology is opaque, the baseline is unspecified, and the comparison is self-reported. This is a meaningful claim being made without auditable grounding.

**The SOC2 Problem**

Starting SOC2 at month eight when targeting enterprise customers is not a minor oversight — it reflects a fundamental misread of the sales environment they claim to be targeting. The pitch frames this as self-aware honesty, but it actually reveals that the team did not understand their own customer for most of their operating history.

**Fundraising and Use of Proceeds**

$2M is thin for the stated goals: SOC2 (expensive and time-consuming), one enterprise sales hire (likely $200-300K+ fully loaded), and one senior infrastructure engineer (similar). The math is tight and leaves little room for the sales cycle length typical in enterprise infrastructure deals.

The Series A thesis — ten enterprise evaluations, two closes, expansion from current cohort — is presented as crisp, but two enterprise closes in 18 months from a standing start, with a first sales hire who hasn't been recruited yet, is an aggressive assumption.

**Switching Costs Argument**

The closing claim that companies don't rip out infrastructure that works cuts both ways. It also means Pulse faces the same barrier getting in. The pitch does not address how they displace existing notification stacks at target accounts, only that once in, they're sticky.

**Team**

There is no team description. Investors are told the retry architecture was built by engineers from Twilio and AWS SNS, but there is no information about who is actually on the team, how many people there are, or what the founding team's relevant experience is beyond two credential drops.

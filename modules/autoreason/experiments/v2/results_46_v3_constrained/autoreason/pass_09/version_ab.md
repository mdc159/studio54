# Pulse — YC Seed Pitch

---

Every engineering team building a consumer app hits the same wall. Somewhere between shipping their core product and scaling to their first million users, they lose three to six months building notification infrastructure. Push, email, SMS, in-app — each channel has its own provider, its own failure modes, its own retry logic. Nobody wants to build it. Everyone does anyway.

We're Pulse. We built the notification layer engineering teams shouldn't have to build themselves.

The competitive landscape is real but specific. AWS SNS is a single channel — you're still stitching the rest together yourself. OneSignal is built for marketers. Knock and Novu have APIs, but their core primitive is a workflow: a template, a trigger, a send. Ours is a delivery guarantee: configurable retry trees per channel, fallback routing when a gateway degrades, and per-notification status engineers can query programmatically. Customers switching from Knock have told us directly they hit a ceiling when they needed programmatic control over delivery logic — we can show you the migration tickets. That structural difference exists because Marcus and Jen between them spent six years at Twilio building carrier retry logic and four years on the AWS SNS team running delivery pipelines at hundreds of billions of notifications annually. That's not a credential line — that's the reason our retry architecture was designed around failure modes from day one, not bolted on later when customers complained.

On delivery reliability: we measure gateway acceptance plus confirmed downstream delivery where carriers return signals. We won't claim a clean apples-to-apples comparison with an industry average that aggregates differently — that would be dishonest. What we can say precisely is our confirmed failure rate is 0.03%, and we'll show you the raw methodology so you can assess the comparison yourself. What we can say without qualification: customers migrating to us report fewer missed transactional alerts. For a million-user app, even a conservative improvement means 60,000 fewer dropped notifications per send cycle. For account access, payment confirmation, delivery status — that's the difference between infrastructure that works and infrastructure that apologizes.

That's both a distribution problem and a customer quality problem. The signal we do have is clean: our first twelve customers have expanded spend as their user bases grew, none have churned, and three enterprise deals are currently stalled on SOC2. That's a self-inflicted mistake we're correcting.

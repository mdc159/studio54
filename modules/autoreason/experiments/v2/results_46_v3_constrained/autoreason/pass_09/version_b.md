# Pulse — YC Seed Pitch (Revised)

---

Every engineering team building a consumer app hits the same wall. Somewhere between shipping their core product and scaling to their first million users, they lose three to six months building notification infrastructure. Push, email, SMS, in-app — each channel has its own provider, its own failure modes, its own retry logic. Nobody wants to build it. Everyone does anyway.

We're Pulse. We built the notification layer engineering teams shouldn't have to build themselves.

The competitive landscape is real but specific. AWS SNS is a single channel. OneSignal is built for marketers. Knock and Novu have APIs, but their core primitive is a workflow: a template, a trigger, a send. We've spoken directly with engineers who migrated from both — the ceiling they hit wasn't a missing feature, it was that adding programmatic delivery control required restructuring their entire integration. We can't claim competitors will never close that gap. We can say Marcus and Jen spent six years at Twilio building carrier retry logic and four years on the AWS SNS team — not at AWS, on that team — and that experience is why our retry architecture was designed around failure modes from day one, not added later when customers complained. That lead is real. Whether it's durable depends on how fast we move, which is why this raise matters.

On delivery reliability: we track gateway acceptance plus confirmed downstream delivery where carriers return signals. We won't claim a clean apples-to-apples comparison with an industry average that aggregates differently — that would be dishonest. What we can say precisely is our confirmed failure rate is 0.03%, and we'll show you the raw methodology. What we can say without qualification: customers migrating to us report fewer dropped transactional alerts. For a million-user app, even a conservative improvement means 60,000 fewer missed notifications per send cycle. For account access, payment confirmation, delivery status — that's infrastructure that works versus infrastructure that apologizes.

Eight months in: 47 paying customers, $38K MRR, 180 million notifications monthly across at least 30 customers with no single account exceeding 20% of volume. We'll be direct about what these numbers mean. $800 average MRR is low, and the honest explanation isn't just that we lacked a sales function — it's that our early positioning attracted smaller teams who weren't yet feeling the scaling pain acutely. That's a customer quality problem, not only a distribution problem. The fix isn't one sales hire; it's SOC2 certification to access enterprise buyers whose pain is proportional to our pricing, combined with a sales hire who has sold developer infrastructure specifically, because that motion is different from growth sales. The signal we do have: our first twelve customers have expanded spend as their user bases grew, none have churned, and the three enterprise deals currently stalled are stalled on SOC2, not on product fit or pricing.

On SOC2: yes, we should have started it earlier. Founders with Twilio and AWS backgrounds should have known enterprise buyers require it. We didn't prioritize it, and we're not going to dress that up. What we can say is that we know exactly what it costs, exactly which deals it unlocks, and it's the first dollar spent from this raise.

We're raising $2M with specific sequencing: SOC2 first, then one enterprise infrastructure sales hire, then one senior engineer. Our 18-month target is $300K MRR. Here's the rough math: SOC2 closes three identified deals averaging $4K monthly — that's $12K added. The sales hire, based on comparable infrastructure sales cycles we've modeled, should yield 8-10 enterprise customers in the first year at $5K-$8K average. Existing customers expanding at current rates contribute another $40K-$50K. That path gets us to $250K-$300K if the hire performs and two of three assumptions hold. It's a model, not a guarantee, and we're showing you the inputs so you can stress-test them.

The switching cost argument is theoretical at our current scale — we know that. Eight months of data isn't a moat. What it is: early evidence that the expansion logic holds where we can observe it. We're asking for $2M to find out honestly whether it compounds.

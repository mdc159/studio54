Here are the real problems:

**The "moat" claim is fragile**

180 million notifications over eight months is not a large operational dataset by infrastructure standards. Twilio processes that volume in hours. The claim that "accumulated operational tuning takes time to replicate" is undermined by the fact that Courier, Knock, and Novu have been running longer and at larger scale. The team's prior experience at Twilio and AWS is cited as the source of the architecture — which means competitors can hire the same profile. Experience isn't proprietary.

**The reliability metric is doing more work than it can support**

"Gateway acceptance plus confirmed downstream delivery where carriers return confirmation signals" is a heavily qualified metric. The 60,000 dropped notifications figure is derived from comparing their numbers to an "industry average" — but that average is never sourced or defined. A migration dataset from 47 customers is not a statistically meaningful basis for this claim, and sophisticated infrastructure buyers will probe exactly this.

**The self-diagnosis of distribution failure is incomplete**

They attribute slow growth to not having a sales function, but 47 customers after eight months with three enterprise deals stalled on a missing SOC2 — a credential any infrastructure vendor would know is required — suggests the go-to-market problems are more fundamental than headcount. The SOC2 gap alone implies they weren't targeting enterprise seriously, which contradicts the pitch's framing around enterprise contract velocity as the Series A thesis.

**The Series A thesis is circular**

They say 18 months is enough time to demonstrate enterprise contract velocity. But the plan is: get SOC2, hire one growth person, hire one engineer. That's a very thin execution surface to generate the kind of enterprise pipeline that justifies a Series A on infrastructure. One growth hire is not a sales organization, and enterprise infrastructure sales cycles routinely exceed 18 months from first contact to closed contract.

**The competitive differentiation is asserted, not demonstrated**

The pitch claims their architecture "reflects an engineer instrumenting a distributed system" versus competitors' product-manager-oriented approach. But this distinction is never made concrete. What specifically does Pulse do that Knock or Courier cannot? The retry logic and fallback architecture are described in general terms that any competitor could claim.

**The expansion revenue signal is too thin to carry the weight placed on it**

"First twelve customers, six-plus months in, have expanded spend, none have churned" is presented as validation of the expansion model. Twelve customers is not a model — it's an anecdote. The expansion could reflect general user base growth rather than anything specific to Pulse's value delivery or pricing structure.

**The $38K MRR against 47 customers implies very small average contract values**

That's roughly $800 average MRR per customer. For infrastructure targeting companies scaling to a million users, this is low — and it raises questions about whether current customers are genuinely in the target band or whether Pulse is serving smaller companies than the pitch implies.

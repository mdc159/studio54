Here are real problems with this proposal:

**The reliability claim is suspicious**

99.97% vs. 94% industry average is presented as a factual comparison, but these numbers almost certainly aren't measuring the same thing. Delivery rate means different things across channels — a push notification "delivered" to a device that never displays it, an email that lands in spam, an SMS that gets carrier-filtered. If Pulse is cherry-picking the channel or definition where they look best, this is misleading. If it's real, there's no explanation of how they achieve it, which makes it unverifiable.

**The customer math doesn't add up**

180 million notifications per month across 47 customers at $0.001 per notification after a 100K free tier. That's roughly 180M × $0.001 = $180K in gross revenue potential, but they're reporting $38K MRR. Either most of their volume is in the free tier, customers are heavily concentrated, or there's significant churn. They don't address this gap, and it implies their monetization is weaker than the volume figure suggests.

**The competitive framing is incomplete**

They dismiss AWS SNS and OneSignal but don't mention Courier, Knock, Novu, Engagespot, or several other developer-focused notification platforms that are direct competitors and well-funded. This either means they don't know about them or are deliberately omitting them. Neither is reassuring.

**SOC2 as the enterprise unlock is a delay admission**

They're already in conversations with three enterprise prospects who can't sign. That means their near-term pipeline is blocked, and the $2M is partially being used to unblock sales that should have been anticipated earlier. This also signals they've been selling into a segment they weren't qualified for.

**The TAM argument is asserted, not demonstrated**

"Every app with real users needs it" is not a market size. There's no number attached to the addressable market, no pricing math showing what the opportunity is worth, and no explanation of why this is a venture-scale business rather than a profitable but small infrastructure tool.

**Team composition is a risk they're glossing over**

Three infrastructure engineers with no one in sales, growth, or product is a specific kind of team that tends to over-engineer and under-distribute. The pitch treats this as a pure strength. At 47 customers after eight months, customer acquisition is the actual constraint, not engineering depth.

**The use of funds is thin**

Two engineers and SOC2 certification for $2M is vague. SOC2 Type II typically costs $30K–$150K. Two senior engineers might be $600K–$800K total fully loaded. That leaves $1M+ unaccounted for, which either means the runway math is being padded or the plan isn't fully formed.

## Problems Found

### 1. The Document Contains Prohibited Preamble Content
The task asks for a policy memo. The document opens with a multi-paragraph editorial section ("I'll address each problem systematically...") listing changes made to a prior draft. This is not part of the policy and should not exist in the deliverable. It is filler that inflates the word count and is not a policy document component.

### 2. Word Count Almost Certainly Exceeds 500 Words
The constraint is a maximum of 500 words. The preamble alone is approximately 200 words. The four policy sections together are well over 350 words. The combined document almost certainly exceeds 500 words by a significant margin. The preamble is not labeled as excluded from the count, and even if it were, the policy itself is borderline.

### 3. Enforcement Item 2 Is Not a Numbered Policy Rule — It Is an Explanation
Enforcement item 2 reads as a justification or explanatory note ("is the control that enforces...") rather than a directive or rule. The constraint requires numbered items, implying each item is a distinct enforceable requirement. This item tells the reader why something works rather than stating what must happen, which is inconsistent with the memo's directive register and arguably constitutes aspirational/explanatory prose embedded in a numbered list.

### 4. Permitted Uses Has No Approved Tool for Sales
Sales staff represent 45% of AI users per the survey, yet the Permitted Uses section contains nothing governing what sales may or may not do with AI tools. There is no prohibition that explicitly covers sales either. The only prohibition touching sales is Prohibition 2, which restricts unreviewed external content, but there is no affirmative permitted use defined for sales. This leaves a significant functional group without actionable guidance in the Permitted Uses section.

### 5. Scope Item 3 References a Survey as a Policy Basis Without Grounding It as Enforceable
Scope item 3 states that the survey "confirms" usage and that this policy "supersedes all prior informal practices." The constraint states every prohibition must reference which base fact motivates it — but Scope items carry no such requirement. However, stating that the policy "supersedes all prior informal practices" is a legal/operational claim that has no enforcement mechanism identified anywhere in the document, making it an assertion rather than an enforceable rule.

### 6. Enforcement Item 1 Conflates Two Different Incident Types
The enforcement item states that violations "involving customer data exposure" trigger the SOC2 incident response procedure. However, the copyright incident (Incident 2) and the GPL license incident (Incident 3) also carry legal exposure. Limiting mandatory incident response to only customer data violations may leave those incidents handled only under the generic disciplinary track in item 3, which may be inconsistent with the company's actual SOC2 obligations and FedRAMP requirements.

### 7. No Mention of FedRAMP-Specific Restrictions in Prohibited Uses
The base facts state FedRAMP authorization is pending with a Q3 target. Outside counsel's DPA finding and FedRAMP obligations are cited in Scope item 4 and Prohibition 1, but no prohibition specifically addresses the heightened restrictions FedRAMP imposes (e.g., on data handling or approved cloud services). This is a gap given the explicit base fact.

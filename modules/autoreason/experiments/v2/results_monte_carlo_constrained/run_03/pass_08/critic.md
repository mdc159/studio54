## Problems Found

### 1. Word Count Violation
The document substantially exceeds 500 words. The policy body alone (everything from "AI Tool Usage Policy" through the final Enforcement item) is well over 500 words — conservatively around 550–600 words excluding the header block. The "Changes made" section at the bottom pushes the total far higher, but even treating that as a separate artifact, the policy itself violates the hard 500-word constraint.

### 2. The "Changes Made" Section Has No Business Being in the Policy Document
The task asks for a policy memo. The appended self-commentary ("Changes made and problems each fixes") is not part of a policy memo. It references "Problems 1–8" from some prior review cycle, which means the document being reviewed is a revision artifact, not a finished deliverable. A policy distributed to employees cannot include internal editorial notes. This is a category error, not a style issue.

### 3. Scope Item 3 Is Filler With No Enforceable Content
"This policy supersedes all prior informal AI tool usage, for which no official policy previously existed" does no legal or operational work. A policy doesn't need to declare itself superseding informal behavior — informal behavior has no policy standing to supersede. This item consumes words without adding an enforceable rule or a required element.

### 4. Permitted Uses Item 3 Creates an Unverifiable Prerequisite
The requirement for "prior written approval from the CTO and Legal, documented before any use begins" for any non-approved tool is stated as a permission pathway, but the policy provides no mechanism to confirm compliance before use occurs. The enforcement section contains no corresponding item for this permission pathway — there is no consequence listed for using a non-approved tool without having obtained this approval separately from Prohibited Use 5. The two items partially overlap without resolving which enforcement action applies.

### 5. Enforcement Item 4 Contains Aspirational Gradation Without Defined Triggers
"First violations result in a written warning and mandatory manager review; repeated violations result in disciplinary action up to and including termination" does not define what constitutes a "repeated" violation (same type? any type? within what timeframe?). More critically, the constraint bars aspirational or vague language. "Up to and including termination" is the canonical example of non-committal disciplinary language — it states a maximum without binding any outcome, making it unenforceable as written.

### 6. Prohibited Use 4 Cites Facts Not Fully Present in the Base Facts
The motivating facts for the Slack prohibition include "unassessed DPA, SOC2, GDPR, and FedRAMP compliance exposure." The base facts state only that Slack AI features are disabled — they do not state that these features are unassessed or that the reason for disabling them is compliance exposure. The policy is adding an inference as if it were a base fact, which violates the constraint that nothing not derivable from the base facts may be added.

### 7. No Section Headers Use Numbered Items in Scope
Scope contains three prose-style declarative sentences. The constraint requires "numbered items (not prose paragraphs)." Numbering sentences that are structurally paragraphs does not satisfy this constraint — Scope items 1, 2, and 3 are each single continuous prose statements, indistinguishable in form from numbered paragraphs.

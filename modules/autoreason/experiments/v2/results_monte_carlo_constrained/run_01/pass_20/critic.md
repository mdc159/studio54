## Problems Found

### 1. The Document Is Not a Policy Memo — It Is a Revision Memo Followed by a Policy

The task asks for a policy memo. The submitted document opens with a multi-paragraph editorial commentary section ("I'll address each problem in turn…") explaining changes made to a prior draft. This preamble is not part of the policy and was not requested. It references "problems" in a previous version that doesn't exist in this submission context. The deliverable is non-conforming in form.

---

### 2. Word Count Cannot Be Verified and Likely Exceeds 500 Words

The policy section alone — excluding the preamble — runs well over 500 words. The Prohibited Uses section alone contains dense numbered items with inline basis citations. The entire document including the preamble is substantially longer. The constraint is "Maximum 500 words" with no carve-out for preamble text. Even if the preamble is excluded, the policy body itself appears to exceed 500 words.

---

### 3. Scope Item 2 Is Not a Scope Statement

Scope is supposed to define who and what the policy applies to. Item 2 ("AI-generated code and content may not be copyrightable; the company makes no ownership assertion over outputs produced by AI tools") is a legal disclaimer or IP position statement, not a scope item. It does not define applicability. Placing it in Scope misuses the section structurally and dilutes the required four-section format.

---

### 4. Scope Item 3 Is Administrative Filler Dressed as Scope

"This policy is enforced using existing access controls…No new tooling is required" is a note about enforcement methodology, not a statement of scope. Scope should define who and what is covered. This item belongs in Enforcement at most, and even there it is padding. It does not identify any person, role, system, or activity boundary.

---

### 5. Permitted Uses Item 2 Prohibition Embedded in a Permitted Uses Section

Item 2 of Permitted Uses states "no IP ownership claim may be asserted over unmodified AI output." This is a prohibition, not a permitted use. Mixing prohibitions into the Permitted Uses section undermines the clarity and enforceability of the four-section structure and makes the policy harder to enforce because violations of this rule are buried in the wrong section.

---

### 6. Enforcement Item 5 Has No Graduated or Proportional Logic — And Creates a Perverse Outcome

Revoking access to "the relevant approved AI tool" for any confirmed violation — including, for example, a sales rep violating the copyright review requirement — may have no meaningful effect if the violating employee never had access to an approved AI tool in the first place. The enforcement action is disconnected from the violation type in those cases, rendering it unenforceable in practice for non-engineering staff.

---

### 7. Enforcement Has No Consequence Beyond Access Revocation and HR Recording

The four enforcement items (2–5) describe reporting, HR notation, manager review, and tool access revocation. None of these constitutes a disciplinary consequence. The policy states violations are "recorded" and "reviewed" but specifies no outcome — no warning, no termination risk, nothing. An enforceable policy requires stated consequences, not just process steps.

---

### 8. "Designated Copyright Reviewer" in Prohibited Uses Item 2 Is an Undefined Role

The policy refers to "Legal or a designated copyright reviewer" without defining who designates this person, what qualifications are required, or where this role is established. This is an invented organizational role not derivable from the base facts, violating the constraint against adding information not derivable from the provided facts.

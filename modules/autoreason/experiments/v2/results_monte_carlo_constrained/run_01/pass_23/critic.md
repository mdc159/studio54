## Problems Found

### 1. The Document Contains Non-Policy Content
The document opens with a multi-paragraph meta-commentary block ("I'll address each problem in turn, then present the revised document" plus eight numbered "changes made" bullets). This is not part of the policy memo. It is revision notes from a prior drafting cycle included verbatim in the submitted document. The task asked for a policy memo, not a revision log.

### 2. Word Count Violation Is Likely
The constraint is a maximum of 500 words. The policy body alone (from "AI Tool Usage Policy" header through the final Enforcement item) substantially exceeds 500 words. A rough count of the policy body text yields approximately 550–600 words, not counting the meta-commentary preamble. The constraint is violated.

### 3. Permitted Uses Item 4 Is Not Enforceable Without New Tooling
Item 4 permits sales staff to use AI writing tools with prior written approval from the Sales department head and Legal. No specific approved tool is named, and the base facts provide no existing access control or review process that governs generic "AI writing tools" used by sales staff. The approval mechanism described is procedural gatekeeping for an open-ended category of unapproved tools, which means enforcement depends on individual compliance rather than any existing control. The constraint requires enforceability through existing access controls and review processes only.

### 4. Prohibited Use Item 5 Basis Is Circular and Partially Fabricated
The stated basis for keeping Slack AI features disabled references DPA terms and FedRAMP, but the base fact is simply that Slack AI features are already disabled — that is the current state, not a reasoned prohibition. More critically, the basis invented for this prohibition (that enabling Slack AI would violate DPA terms) is not directly derivable from the base facts, which state only that inputting customer data into third-party AI services likely violates DPA terms. Slack's AI features being disabled is an existing configuration, not a DPA-derived prohibition, and the memo conflates the two.

### 5. Permitted Uses Item 5 Budget Approval Process Is Not Enforceable Without New Tooling
Item 5 requires the approving department head and Legal to "confirm available budget against the $50K/year AI tooling limit before granting approval." No existing process for tracking cumulative AI tool spend in real time is derivable from the base facts. This creates an enforcement dependency on a budget-tracking mechanism that is not established in the base facts and is not an existing access control or review process.

### 6. Prohibited Use Item 6 Exceeds the Base Facts
The prohibition against asserting copyright ownership over "unmodified AI output" introduces a distinction ("unmodified") that does not appear in the base facts. Outside counsel flagged only that AI-generated code may not be copyrightable — no qualifier about modification was mentioned. The word "unmodified" imports a legal distinction not derivable from the stated facts.

### 7. Enforcement Item 4 Lacks a Named Responsible Party
"Manager review is mandatory" does not specify which manager — the violating employee's direct manager, Engineering leadership, the Sales department head, or another. This makes the item ambiguous and therefore not concretely enforceable.

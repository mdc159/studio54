## Problems Found

### 1. The document includes a preamble that is not part of the policy memo

The task asks for a policy memo. The document opens with "I'll address each problem systematically, then present the revised document" followed by a bulleted list of self-described changes. This is not part of the memo and violates the deliverable format. The task did not ask for a revision log or commentary. This preamble content counts toward the word total and pollutes the document.

### 2. Word count likely exceeds 500 words

The constraint is a maximum of 500 words. The preamble alone runs approximately 280 words. The policy body runs approximately 430 words. Combined the document is well over 700 words. Even if the preamble is excluded, the policy body itself is close to or at the limit and needs verification, but the presence of the preamble makes the overall document a clear violation regardless.

### 3. Scope item 1 contains a definition embedded in prose

The constraint requires numbered items, not prose paragraphs. Scope item 1 defines "customer data" inside a paragraph. This is a prose definition embedded within what should be a discrete numbered item. It functions as a prose paragraph with a definitional clause bolted on, not a clean numbered policy item.

### 4. Permitted Uses item 3 addresses budget governance, not a permitted use

The Permitted Uses section should enumerate what employees are allowed to do. Item 3 describes budget allocation and a future approval process. Neither of these is a permitted use by an employee. It is administrative/governance content misclassified into the wrong section.

### 5. Enforcement item 2 conflates detection mechanism with violation consequence

The constraint requires enforceable items. Enforcement item 2 says violations "are detected through" certain processes — this describes how violations might be found, not an enforcement action. Detection and enforcement are different things. Describing a detection method as an enforcement measure is not an enforceable policy statement.

### 6. The 45% of sales using AI for email drafting is unaddressed

The base facts state 45% of sales staff are already using AI tools for email drafting. Incident #2 directly involves a sales rep. The policy's Permitted Uses section covers only engineers with GitHub Copilot Business seats. Sales employees have no permitted AI tool and no guidance specific to their existing behavior. The policy does not explicitly address what sales staff may or may not use in a way that acknowledges the documented usage pattern, leaving a known enforcement gap unaddressed.

### 7. Prohibition 2's motivating fact citations are inaccurate for the prohibition stated

Prohibition 2 bans unapproved tools for all employees. Incident #2 (competitor copyrighted text) and Incident #3 (GPL header) are cited as motivating facts. But those incidents motivate IP risk from AI output, not the general prohibition on unapproved tools. The actual motivating facts for prohibiting unapproved tools would be the DPA violation risk and the lack of enterprise data controls — neither of which is cited here.

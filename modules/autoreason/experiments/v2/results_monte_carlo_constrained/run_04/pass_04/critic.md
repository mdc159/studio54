## Problems Identified

### Constraint Violations

**Word count:** The document substantially exceeds 500 words. The body text alone (excluding headers and formatting) is well over 500 words. This is a direct violation of the stated maximum.

**Aspirational/non-enforceable language in Enforcement item 4:** "Managers address AI tool compliance with direct reports during existing quarterly performance reviews" is not enforceable — it describes an intention or expectation, not a defined action triggered by a specific condition. It functions as aspirational language, which the constraints prohibit.

**Enforcement section has 6 numbered items, not 4:** The constraint requires exactly 4 sections with numbered items, but does not cap the number of items per section. However, this is worth flagging because Enforcement items 4 and 5 add procedural expectations that lack clear triggers or responsible parties, creating enforceability ambiguity.

### Factual / Derivability Problems

**Permitted Uses item 4 implies a process that doesn't exist in the base facts.** The base facts state a $50K budget is allocated but do not establish any approval process or workflow. The memo presents a specific submission procedure ("Requests must be submitted to Legal and Engineering leadership") as if it is an existing mechanism, but this is invented content not derivable from the base facts.

**Prohibited Uses item 3 misstates the motivating fact.** The legal flag from outside counsel was specifically about inputting customer data into third-party AI services violating DPA terms — not about copyright ownership of AI-generated content generally. Attributing "copyright ownership risk" to outside counsel's flagged concern is an unsupported extension.

**Prohibited Uses item 2 cites FedRAMP as a motivating fact for banning unapproved tools broadly**, but the base facts only state FedRAMP authorization is pending — they do not specify that unapproved AI tool usage is a FedRAMP compliance requirement. This is an inference not directly derivable from the stated facts.

**Scope item 3 references Slack AI features** implicitly through "AI features embedded in existing platforms," but company Slack has AI features disabled — meaning there is nothing to prohibit or govern there. Covering it under scope without acknowledging it is already disabled creates ambiguity about whether employees need to take any action.

### Missing Required Elements

**No prohibition explicitly covers Slack AI features** despite the base fact that they are currently disabled (implying a risk that they could be re-enabled or accessed). The policy does not address who controls that setting or what happens if it changes.

**The 80-seat cap enforcement mechanism is undefined.** Permitted Uses item 1 says seat allocation is managed by Engineering leadership, but there is no enforcement item specifying what happens when demand exceeds 80 seats or how overages are detected using existing controls.

### Unnecessary Filler

**Scope item 3's second sentence** ("Prior undocumented use does not constitute permission") is redundant — a policy superseding prior practices already implies this. It adds words without adding enforceable content.

**Enforcement item 6** restates the obvious consequence of any policy violation and adds no information specific to AI tool usage. It consumes words without substantive content unique to this policy.

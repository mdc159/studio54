## REAL PROBLEMS WITH THIS HYBRID WORK POLICY

### **OPERATIONAL COMPLEXITY THAT DOESN'T DELIVER VALUE**

**Meeting Scheduling Is Unworkable**
- The "Core meeting hours 11 AM - 3 PM PT" creates a 4-hour daily window for a 53-person company across multiple timezones. This will create meeting gridlock.
- EU rotation system (50% live, 50% async) requires someone to manually track and enforce rotation schedules - pure administrative overhead with no clear owner.
- "Quarterly calibration sessions" for 53 people across departments will consume enormous management time for marginal benefit.

**Equipment Policy Over-Engineering**
- $500 setup + $50/month internet + $200/year supplies + $400 chair reimbursement = $1,350+ per remote employee in year one. For a company this size, that's $70K+ annually just in stipends, before considering tracking and reimbursement administration.
- "IT support: Remote diagnostics within 4 hours, replacement within 24 hours" requires dedicated IT staffing that's unrealistic for 53 employees.

### **ASSUMPTIONS THAT DON'T MATCH REALITY**

**Departmental Needs Mismatch**
- Engineering "1 day per month" assumes all engineering work is equally suited to remote collaboration. Complex architecture decisions, code reviews, and knowledge transfer often need real-time interaction.
- Sales "Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday" assumes all clients operate on this schedule. Enterprise sales cycles and client availability don't conform to arbitrary internal schedules.

**Geographic Distribution Ignored**
- Policy assumes EU team (3 people) can meaningfully participate in "alternating months" for all-hands meetings. This creates a two-class system where EU employees miss critical company communications 50% of the time.
- "No-meeting blocks after 6 PM ET" means West Coast employees lose 3 hours of potential collaboration time with East Coast colleagues.

### **PERFORMANCE FRAMEWORK DISCONNECTED FROM OPERATIONS**

**Measurement Without Baseline**
- "Outcomes and impact" evaluation assumes the company already has clear, measurable outcomes defined for each role. Most 53-person companies don't have this level of performance clarity.
- "360-degree feedback including remote/in-office collaboration" requires a feedback system that doesn't exist and significant HR infrastructure to administer.

**Manager Training Overreach**
- "Remote team management certification within 90 days" assumes such certifications exist, are valuable, and that managers have bandwidth for additional training requirements.

### **MISSING CRITICAL OPERATIONAL PIECES**

**Office Space Economics Ignored**
- Policy requires office space for "hot-desking stations" and "dedicated desks for Sales team" but doesn't address lease obligations, space utilization costs, or what happens when office requirements exceed physical space.
- No consideration of office location accessibility for employees who might live far from office locations.

**Client and Business Impact Blind Spots**
- Sales team "3 days per week" requirement ignores that client meetings often drive schedule needs, not internal collaboration preferences.
- No consideration of how customer-facing commitments (support calls, client presentations, demos) interact with rigid office schedules.

**Technology Infrastructure Gaps**
- "Professional lighting/audio for remote participants" in hybrid meetings assumes office conference rooms are equipped for this, which requires significant upfront investment not addressed in budget.
- Policy assumes reliable internet and power at employee homes without addressing backup plans for outages.

### **GOVERNANCE AND ENFORCEMENT PROBLEMS**

**Exception Process Unscalable**
- "Exception requests submitted to department head" creates bottleneck and inconsistent application across departments.
- "Performance improvement plans may require increased office presence" creates legal risk by potentially discriminating against employees based on location preference.

**Feedback Mechanisms Over-Designed**
- "Monthly anonymous surveys, quarterly focus groups" for 53 people will generate more administrative work than actionable insights.
- No clear process for who analyzes feedback, makes decisions, or implements changes.

### **TIMELINE REALITY CHECK**

**Implementation Assumes Perfect Coordination**
- "IT equipment orders and delivery" in weeks 1-2 assumes all employees need equipment simultaneously and that supply chains can deliver within this timeline.
- "Office space reconfiguration" in 2 weeks assumes landlord approval, contractor availability, and no permitting requirements.
- "Manager training begins" assumes training programs exist and managers have capacity during the same period they're implementing major operational changes.

The policy tries to solve too many problems simultaneously while creating new administrative burdens that will consume management attention without clear business benefit.

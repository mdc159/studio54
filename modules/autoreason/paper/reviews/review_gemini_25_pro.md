## Summary
This paper introduces "autoreason," an iterative refinement method for large language models (LLMs) working on subjective tasks where objective metrics are unavailable. The method operates in a loop where an incumbent document (A) is challenged by an adversarial revision (B) and a synthesis of the two (AB). The winner is determined by a blind panel of fresh LLM judges using Borda count aggregation, and this winner becomes the incumbent for the next round. The authors claim that with a moderately capable model (claude-sonnet-4), this iterative process significantly outperforms single-pass methods and other iterative baselines on several subjective tasks. However, they present a crucial negative result: with a more capable model (claude-sonnet-4.6), the method fails to converge on open-ended tasks because the synthesis operator consistently produces superior outputs. The paper's primary contribution is identifying that this failure is due to unbounded task "scope," and demonstrating that by imposing explicit scope constraints, the method's effectiveness and convergence properties are restored even with the stronger model.

## Strengths (be specific, cite sections)
1.  **Novel and Important Finding on Model Scaling and Task Scope:** The paper's most significant contribution is the investigation in Section~\ref{sec:scaling}. It is rare and valuable to see a paper that so thoroughly investigates a failure mode of its own method. The systematic approach—observing the failure with a stronger model (Table~\ref{tab:scaling}), attempting and analyzing evaluation-side remedies (Section 5.1), and ultimately identifying task scope as the root cause (Section 5.2)—is exemplary. The contrast shown in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling_story} is a powerful and clear illustration of the core finding: that the interaction between the evaluation method and the task specification is critical, especially as model capabilities increase. This is a timely and important insight for the field.

2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a commendably thorough set of experiments. The use of five distinct tasks, a 7-judge panel for final evaluation, and multiple baselines provides a solid foundation for the claims in Section~\ref{sec:v1}. Furthermore, the robustness analysis in Section~\ref{sec:retro} is a major strength. The Monte Carlo analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:mc}) demonstrates the stability of the final output quality, and the game-theoretic validation (confirming transitivity and Elo plateaus) provides strong evidence that the process is not just randomly cycling but is indeed converging towards a stable equilibrium. This level of analysis goes well beyond what is typical and significantly increases confidence in the method's dynamics.

3.  **Clear Methodological Presentation and Practical Insights:** The autoreason method itself is clearly explained and motivated. Figure~\ref{fig:method} is an excellent, easy-to-understand diagram of the process. The design choices, such as using fresh, isolated agents for each role and a blind judge panel, are well-justified by prior work on LLM biases. The paper also provides several immediately useful, practical insights. The finding that Chain-of-Thought judging accelerates convergence by 3x (Section~\ref{sec:cot}, Table~\ref{tab:cot}) is a simple but powerful trick. Similarly, the meta-experiment on writing the paper itself (Section~\ref{sec:paper}) provides a compelling demonstration of the need for a ground-truth-aware critic to combat hallucination (Figure~\ref{fig:hallucination}).

## Weaknesses (be specific, cite sections)
1.  **Undefined Baselines:** This is a critical omission that undermines the paper's central empirical claims. The paper repeatedly compares autoreason to four baselines: "conservative," "improve\_this," "harsh\_critic," and "critique\_and\_revise" (e.g., Tables~\ref{tab:v1}, \ref{tab:multi}, \ref{tab:scaling}). However, the paper never defines what these baselines are or how they were implemented. Are they single-turn prompts? Are they iterative loops? If iterative, what is the mechanism? For example, is "critique\_and\_revise" a single agent critiquing its own work, similar to Self-Refine? Without this information, the results in Tables 1, 2, 5, 6, 7, and 10 are not interpretable or reproducible. The claims of superiority are rendered unverifiable.
    *   **Impact:** This is a major flaw. A reviewer cannot assess whether the comparisons are fair or meaningful.
    *   **Suggested Fix:** Add a subsection in Section 3 (Experiments) or an appendix that precisely defines each baseline method. For prompt-based methods, include the prompts. For iterative methods, describe the loop structure.

2.  **Limited Generalization of the Scope-Constraint Solution:** The key finding from Section~\ref{sec:scaling} hinges on the success of a single "constrained task" (a 500-word startup pitch). While the result is compelling for that one task (Table~\ref{tab:constrained_quality}), it is a significant leap to conclude that scope constraint is a general solution to the scaling problem. The nature of the constraint (a word count limit and fixed facts) might be particularly well-suited to this specific task, but it's unclear how this principle would apply to other subjective domains, such as writing a policy document or a piece of creative fiction, where hard constraints might be less natural.
    *   **Impact:** The paper's main takeaway, while plausible, rests on a single data point, limiting the perceived generality of the findings.
    *   **Suggested Fix:** The authors should run the constrained experiment on at least one more of their original five tasks (e.g., by adding a word count and section structure requirement to the "remote work policy" task). Even without new experiments, the Discussion should more explicitly temper this claim, framing it as strong evidence from a single case study that warrants future investigation across a broader range of tasks and constraint types.

3.  **Lack of Justification for Key Hyperparameters:** The method relies on several key design choices that are presented without justification or sensitivity analysis. Specifically: the judge panel size is fixed at `n=3` for the loop, the convergence threshold is `k=2` consecutive wins for the incumbent, and Borda count is used for aggregation. Why `n=3` and not 5, or even 1? The paper notes that a broken judge prevents equilibrium (Figure~\ref{fig:integrity}), suggesting sensitivity to `n`. Why `k=2` and not 1 or 3? The analysis of Task 1 (Section 4.3) shows post-convergence passes are noise, but doesn't justify `k=2` as the optimal threshold.
    *   **Impact:** It is unclear how sensitive the method's performance and convergence properties are to these specific choices, making it harder to understand the method's robustness.
    *   **Suggested Fix:** Add a short paragraph to the Discussion or Appendix addressing these choices. Even a brief justification (e.g., "We chose n=3 as a balance between cost and the robustness benefits shown by Condorcet's jury theorem. We found k=1 led to premature termination on noisy judgments, while k=2 provided a stable stopping point in all tested runs.") would be sufficient. An ablation study would be ideal but is not strictly necessary if the choices can be well-motivated.

## Questions for Authors
1.  Could you please provide the precise definitions and, where applicable, the prompts used for the baseline methods ("conservative," "improve\_this," "harsh\_critic," and "critique\_and\_revise")? This is essential for evaluating the paper's main results.

2.  The cost of autoreason is noted as 5 LLM calls per pass. How does the total cost (in calls or tokens) to reach convergence compare with the cost of running the baselines for 15 passes? A cost-benefit analysis would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

3.  In the scaling experiments (Section~\ref{sec:scaling}), the failure mode is that the synthesis (AB) is consistently better than the incumbent (A). This prevents convergence. Have you considered a simpler alternative where only A and B compete, and the winner is chosen? This would be a 3-call loop (critic, author B, judge panel) instead of 5. How do you think this would affect the dynamics, particularly the "bloat" and "drift" you observed?

4.  The choice of `k=2` for the convergence threshold seems heuristic. Your analysis of Task 1 showed that the document at pass 15 was preferred to the one at pass 25. Does this suggest that a lower `k` or an alternative stopping rule based on Elo rating plateaus (mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:retro}) might be more effective at finding the optimal stopping point?

## Minor Issues
*   The citations for SlopCodeBench and ACE point to future years (2026). These should be corrected.
*   In the captions for Figures 1, 4, 5, etc., the figure number is referenced again in parentheses at the end of the caption (e.g., "caption text (Figure X)"). This is redundant and should be removed.
*   The prompts in Appendix~\ref{app:prompts} are excellent, but the prompts for the baseline methods are missing, which relates to the major weakness identified above.
*   The model name "claude-sonnet-4-20250514" is very specific, while "claude-sonnet-4-6" is used for the stronger model. Is the latter a shorthand? Clarifying the model versioning would aid reproducibility.
*   In Table~\ref{tab:remedies}, the `Baseline` row has "30+" wins for AB. What does the "+" signify? Was the run stopped at 50 passes with AB having won more than 30 times? Please clarify.

## Overall Assessment
**Weak Accept.**

This is a very good paper with a significant and timely contribution. The core finding—that iterative refinement with more capable models fails due to unbounded task scope, not flawed evaluation—is an important lesson for the community. The experimental methodology is largely rigorous, especially the inclusion of robustness checks and the systematic investigation of the scaling failure. The paper is well-written, the figures are clear, and the practical takeaways are valuable.

However, the paper is held back from a clear "Accept" by one major, correctable flaw: the complete lack of definition for the baseline methods. This makes it impossible to verify the central performance claims, which are the foundation for the entire investigation. It is a surprising oversight in an otherwise thorough paper.

**The single most important thing the authors should address is to provide clear, precise definitions and implementations for all baseline methods.** If the authors can add this information in a rebuttal or revision, I would be happy to raise my score to a clear "Accept." The other weaknesses are less critical and can be addressed with minor text changes to temper claims and justify hyperparameters. The core research is strong, and with this key piece of information filled in, the paper would represent a solid contribution to NeurIPS/ICML.

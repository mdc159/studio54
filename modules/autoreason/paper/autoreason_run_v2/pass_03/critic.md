## Problems Identified

### Overstated or Insufficiently Qualified Claims

1. **Abstract**: "autoreason ranked first or second in every 7-judge blind comparison, winning 3/5 outright" — this is only demonstrated with Sonnet 4 on 5 tasks. The abstract buries the Sonnet 4.6 failure and then pivots to a single constrained task to rescue the narrative. The framing still oversells the generality of the method.

2. **Abstract**: "game-theoretic analysis suggests near-transitive preferences" — the paper itself acknowledges (Section 5.2) that same-model judges sharing biases could produce apparent transitivity. Calling this a "game-theoretic analysis" in the abstract elevates what is essentially a consistency check on a biased evaluation mechanism.

3. **Section 4.2 (Qualitative Improvement)**: Claims like "quantified pain ($15K/incident × 6/yr)" and "validation from 50+ customer interviews with 75% pilot success rate" are presented as improvements, but these are fabricated numbers generated by an LLM. The paper treats hallucinated specificity as evidence of quality improvement without acknowledging that these "improvements" are fictional details that sound more convincing but have no grounding in reality.

4. **Conclusion**: "Eight remedy experiments point to scope as the root cause rather than evaluation" — this is presented with more certainty than the evidence warrants. The paper tested one constrained task. The eight remedy experiments show that evaluation-side fixes didn't work on one unconstrained task, but that doesn't constitute strong evidence that scope is *the* root cause in general.

### Reviewer Feedback Likely Not Fully Addressed

5. **No human evaluation whatsoever.** The limitations section acknowledges this, but the entire paper's quality claims rest on LLM-as-judge evaluation. The paper evaluates an LLM-based method using LLM judges from the same model family. This circularity is acknowledged but not mitigated in any experimental way. A reviewer asking for even a small-scale human evaluation would find nothing here.

6. **Statistical rigor remains weak.** Monte Carlo has n=5. Scaling remedies each ran once. No confidence intervals, no significance tests. The paper acknowledges these limitations textually but doesn't address them experimentally. The "80% convergence" claim from 4/5 runs is essentially meaningless statistically.

7. **Single model family.** All core experiments use Anthropic models. The one attempt at cross-model evaluation (Opus + Sonnet + Gemini judge panel in Section 6) failed due to a parser issue and was abandoned rather than fixed. This leaves a major generalizability gap.

### Structural and Presentation Issues

8. **Section 2 (Method)**: The formal framing subsection introduces a Markov chain interpretation and invokes the Condorcet jury theorem, then immediately undermines both ("same-model judges share systematic biases, bounding effective independence below n" and "loosely analogous rather than a direct equivalence" re: PSRO). This theoretical apparatus does little analytical work and mostly serves as decoration.

9. **Table 3 (CoT results)**: Reports convergence passes for only 2 tasks, with one cell marked "---". This is thin evidence for the claim that "CoT judges should be the default" (Discussion section). The 3× speedup is from a single task.

10. **Section 5 flow**: The scaling section narrates two rounds of remedy experiments in a chronological discovery format. While transparent, this reads as a lab notebook rather than a structured analysis. The key finding (scope matters) could be presented more directly rather than through 3 tables of negative results.

11. **Figure/table density**: The paper has 11 figures and 9 tables in the main text. Several are small and could be consolidated. Figures 7 (hallucination) and 8 (judge integrity) each make a single binary point that could be stated in a sentence.

### Prose Issues

12. **Section 1**: "analogous to how science uses peer review where math can use proofs" — this analogy is doing a lot of work and is somewhat misleading. Peer review is not a fitness function; it's a gatekeeping mechanism. The comparison obscures more than it clarifies.

13. **Section 4.1**: "iteration inverts the ranking" (Table 2 caption) — this is imprecise. Autoreason goes from 3rd to 1st on average, but critique-and-revise also performs well iteratively (wins 2/5 tasks). The inversion is partial.

14. **Discussion**: "The broader insight: the obstacle to iterative LLM self-improvement is not model capability but the interaction between evaluation methodology and task specification." This is presented as a general insight but is supported by experiments on one model family, one constrained task, and five unconstrained business-writing tasks.

### Missing Details and Methodological Concerns

15. **Baseline fairness**: Baselines get 15 passes × 1 call = 15 calls. Autoreason gets ~60–150 calls. The paper acknowledges the ~6× compute gap but never tests what happens if baselines get 6× more passes (i.e., 90 passes) to equalize compute. This is a significant confound.

16. **Temperature choices**: Section 2 states "these values were chosen based on preliminary runs and not formally ablated." This is honest, but temperature could meaningfully affect both revision diversity and judge consistency, making it a potential confound for all reported results.

17. **Bibliography**: References [2] (SlopCodeBench) and [3] (ACE) are dated 2026, explained by a footnote saying they are "recent preprints available at the time of submission." This is unusual and may raise credibility concerns. Reference [9] (LLM Council) is attributed to Karpathy with minimal bibliographic detail.

18. **The constrained task is a different task**: The paper's central scaling claim compares autoreason on Task 2 (notification system design) vs. a completely different task (500-word startup pitch). This is not a controlled comparison — it changes both the scope constraint and the task content simultaneously. The paper doesn't adequately flag this confound.
